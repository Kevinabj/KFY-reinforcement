    % ---------- Introduction ----------
    \begin{block}{1. Introduction}
        We re-implemented four canonical deep RL algorithms from scratch in PyTorch and benchmarked them on four classic-control Gymnasium environments with three seeds each. The algorithms span the two main axes of the deep RL taxonomy:
        \begin{itemize}
            \item value-based vs.\ policy-based,
            \item on-policy vs.\ off-policy.
        \end{itemize}
        We report bootstrap 95\% confidence intervals over seeds, pre-register four ablations, and document the implementation details that distinguish a paper recipe from a working agent.
    \end{block}

    % ---------- Algorithms ----------
    \begin{block}{2. Algorithms}

        \textbf{\hl{DQN}} \cite{mnih2013playing} (value-based, off-policy, discrete):
        \[
        \mathcal{L}_{\text{DQN}} = \mathbb{E}\!\left[\ell_{\text{Huber}}\!\Big(Q_\theta(s,a) - \big(r + \gamma\,(1{-}d)\,\textstyle\max_{a'} Q_{\bar{\theta}}(s',a')\big)\Big)\right].
        \]
        $\varepsilon$-greedy exploration, target network synced every $\tau_{\text{sync}}$ steps, Huber loss for robustness.

        \vspace{0.6em}
        \textbf{\hl{PPO}} \cite{schulman2017proximal} (policy-based, on-policy, discrete + continuous):
        \[
        \mathcal{L}_{\text{PPO}} = -\,\mathbb{E}\!\left[\min\!\big(r_t(\theta)\hat{A}_t,\, \mathrm{clip}(r_t(\theta), 1{-}\epsilon, 1{+}\epsilon)\hat{A}_t\big)\right].
        \]
        Clipped surrogate as a first-order trust-region; GAE advantages with $(\gamma,\lambda)$.

        \vspace{0.6em}
        \textbf{\hl{SAC}} \cite{haarnoja2018soft} (policy-based, off-policy, continuous):
        \[
        J_{\text{SAC}}(\pi) = \mathbb{E}_\pi\!\left[\textstyle\sum_t \gamma^t\big(r(s_t,a_t) + \alpha\,\mathcal{H}[\pi(\cdot{\mid}s_t)]\big)\right].
        \]
        Squashed-Gaussian actor with tanh-Jacobian log-prob correction; auto-tuned $\alpha$ via $\log\alpha$.

        \vspace{0.6em}
        \textbf{\hl{TD3}} \cite{fujimoto2018addressing} (policy-based, off-policy, continuous):
        \[
        y = r + \gamma\,(1{-}d)\,\textstyle\min_{i\in\{1,2\}}\, Q_{\bar{\phi}_i}\!\big(s',\, \mathrm{clip}(\pi_{\bar{\theta}}(s') + \tilde{\epsilon},\, a_{\min}, a_{\max})\big).
        \]
        Twin Q-targets, target policy smoothing ($\tilde{\sigma}{=}0.2$), delayed actor update every 2 critic steps.

    \end{block}

Code: PyTorch from scratch, no \texttt{stable-baselines3}.
    \end{block}

        \begin{figure}
            \centering

        \end{figure}
        \vspace{-0.6em}
        \small

        \end{center}
        \normalsize
        \vspace{-0.3em}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{CartPole.} \hl{DQN bimodal} (1/3 seeds solve); PPO reliable (380, CI [350, 402]).
            \item \textbf{Acrobot.} DQN solves in $\sim$30k steps; PPO slower, one seed collapses.
            \item \textbf{Pendulum.} \hl{SAC and TD3 are ${\sim}13\times$ more sample-efficient than PPO} (SAC reaches threshold in 7.4k env steps vs PPO 95k).
            \item \textbf{MountainCarContinuous.} \hl{Only PPO ever discovers the +100 goal} (1/3 seeds). SAC ($-0.8$) and TD3 ($-9.1$) plateau at a lazy local optimum.
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}

    % ---------- Ablations / headline finding ----------
    \begin{block}{5. Headline finding: off-policy continuous methods have a structural exploration gap on sparse reward}
        Two pre-registered ablations on MountainCarContinuous test whether the available exploration knobs rescue SAC or TD3. \hl{Neither does.}

        \vspace{0.3em}
        \begin{columns}[T]
            \column{0.48\colwidth}
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=0.48\colwidth]{../plots/ablations/sac_alpha.pdf}\\
            \small \textbf{SAC temperature sweep.} Auto, 0.05, 0.20. \\ Three failure modes, \hl{0 goal discoveries / 9 seeds}.
            \column{0.48\colwidth}
            \centering
            \includegraphics[width=0.48\colwidth]{../plots/ablations/td3_exploration_noise.pdf}\\
            \small \textbf{TD3 noise sweep.} $\sigma \in \{0.1, 0.3, 0.5\}$. \\ \hl{More noise is worse, not better.}
        \end{columns}

        \vspace{0.6em}
        \textbf{Mechanism.} Both off-policy mechanisms (entropy regularization, additive Gaussian noise) drive uniform coverage of the \emph{local} action set, not directed exploration in state space. PPO's on-policy rollouts occasionally produce a long correlated action sequence that crosses the hill; off-policy replay samples are decorrelated and cannot.

        \vspace{0.4em}
        \textbf{Other ablations confirm the diary.} DQN target-sync sweep is a U-shape ($\tau\in\{100, 1000, 5000\}$), explaining CartPole bimodality: $\tau{=}1000$ sits at the edge of stability. PPO clip ratio: $0.1$ is too conservative; $0.2$ and $0.3$ are tied.
    \end{block}

    % ---------- Takeaways ----------
    \begin{block}{6. Answers to the brief's four questions}
        \begin{itemize}
            \item \textbf{Most expensive per iteration:} SAC (1183 to 2713\,s per 100k steps) $>$ TD3 $>$ PPO $>$ DQN (98 to 120\,s).
            \item \textbf{Most compact policy:} DQN \& PPO ($\sim$4.6k actor params) vs SAC \& TD3 ($\sim$67k). Caveat: this gap follows our $(64,64)$ vs $(256,256)$ width choice, not the algorithms.
            \item \textbf{Best on continuous actions:} \hl{no single winner}. SAC/TD3 dominate dense reward (Pendulum); PPO is the only one to solve sparse reward (MCC).
            \item \textbf{Best off-policy data efficiency:} SAC, by $12.9\times$ over PPO on Pendulum (7.4k vs 95k steps to threshold).
        \end{itemize}
    \end{block}
